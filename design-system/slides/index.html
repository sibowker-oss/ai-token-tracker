Hepburn Advisory
Context · 1 of 2

Three things are simultaneously true, and the market is pricing only one of them.

One. The capital commitments are real. $745B of cumulative capex through 2026, sourced directly from hyperscaler filings and Tier 1 issuer disclosures.

Two. Booked AI revenue is ~$22B trailing twelve months — a figure that includes every line item issuers are willing to attribute to AI, generously interpreted.

Three. The implied payback period, at current growth rates, exceeds the useful life of the depreciating asset. This is the reconciliation the market has not yet made.

The capex is a sunk cost. The question is what reconciles it. — Hepburn house view, Q1 '26

Framing

How issuers describe the moment — and what the numbers actually say.

Issuer narrative

“Compute-bound, revenue-unconstrained.”

Forward ARR guidance is framed in terms of capacity: more chips, more power, more tokens served. The constraint is always supply-side.

Under this frame, the capex is investment against a queue of unmet demand. The payback window is assumed, not demonstrated.

Hepburn reconciliation

Revenue growth is real, but not at the shape required.

Booked AI revenue is growing ~85% YoY on our numbers. At that rate, sustained for 36 months, the gap closes to ~5×.

That is still the widest capex/revenue ratio in any comparable cycle. The cycle is not broken. It is merely expensive.

Recommendations

Four moves, sequenced across the next two board cycles.

✓

Reclassify AI line items by what they're actually paying for.

Split the P&L into outcome, integration, and optionality. The first gets the budget; the second gets scrutiny; the third gets a kill-date.

✓

Establish the reconciliation cadence before the market forces it.

Monthly capex-to-revenue ratio, reported internally, tiered by source. The goal is to see the trend before it is a press cycle.

✓

Map vendor concentration against credible downside.

If any single hyperscaler cuts capex 20% in 2027, what breaks? The answer should be on one page, not in an audit document.

✓

Rebuild the category-authority plan around the reconciled view.

Stop pitching into the vendor narrative. The differentiation is the reconciliation — show your work, publish the numbers.

Hepburn view · Analyst note

The SaaS implementation tax, re-skinned as AI transformation.

The vendor sells outcomes. The buyer still pays for integration, change management, and the quarterly reconciliation of what the model was supposed to automate against what it actually did. That gap is the tax.

Every prior platform wave — client-server, web, mobile, cloud — ran the same arithmetic. AI is not different in kind. It is different in magnitude. And the magnitude is, so far, in the cost column.

Framework · Pick one

The reconciliation has to come from somewhere.

Path of least pain

Revenue expands ~4× over 36 months

  • Enterprise seat penetration triples from 18% to ~55%
  • Per-seat pricing holds; no discount pressure
  • Capex plateau; no new multi-year commitments
  • Implied CAGR: 58% · precedented in zero comparable cycles
Paths of more pain

Capex contracts, or margins compress

  • Hyperscaler capex cuts → chip oversupply → margin shock
  • Lab revenue grows but at commodity API pricing
  • Either path requires a recession the market hasn't priced
  • Board planning horizon: 12 months, not 36
