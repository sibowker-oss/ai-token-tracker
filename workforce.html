Already Happening: Real-World Examples

The Uncomfortable Math

If AI revenue reaches $500B by 2030 and 60% of that displaces labor, that's $300B/year in human work replaced by tokens. At a blended $100K/employee, that's 3 million full-time equivalent roles globally. At the bull case ($1T revenue, 80% displacement), it's 8+ million roles.

The paradox: the only way the $250B+ capex investment pays off is if AI becomes good enough to replace substantial human work at scale. The investors need workforce displacement to earn their returns. The capex gap isn't a technology problem — it's a bet that AI will eliminate enough jobs, fast enough, to justify the infrastructure.

Or: the capex doesn't pay off, the bubble deflates, and fewer jobs are displaced. You can't have it both ways.

The Counter-Argument: New Value, Not Just Substitution

Not all AI spend displaces labor. Some creates entirely new capabilities: personalized education at scale, drug discovery acceleration, real-time translation, accessibility tools, creative augmentation. If 40% of AI revenue creates new value rather than substituting existing work, the displacement numbers shrink significantly.

History also shows that automation creates new job categories that didn't exist before. ATMs didn't eliminate bank tellers — banks opened more branches and tellers shifted to advisory roles. The question is whether the transition happens fast enough for workers to adapt, and whether the new roles pay as well as the displaced ones.
