Methodology

How we build the numbers, where they come from, and how confident we are.

Methodology caveat

Projections are directional arithmetic, not forecasts. Benchmarks are dated and sourced. Each derived metric defaults to a documented method; every assumption is editable. Where a number appears on a Ledger page, that page is the canonical source — this page describes how those numbers are built, not what they are at any given moment.

The AI Ledger tracks the AI industry through three interlocking ledgers. The Capital Ledger follows how much is being spent to build AI infrastructure — semiconductors, data centres, networking. The Revenue Ledger tracks what comes back — who earns what from selling AI products and services. The Usage Ledger estimates actual consumption — tokens processed, models served, inference volume. Together they answer: how much is going in, how much is coming out, and how much is actually being used?

Per-Ledger Methodology

Capital

Capital Ledger

Anchored from NVIDIA Data Centre revenue (Tier 1A), which sets the silicon floor. Cross-checked against MSFT, GOOG, META, and AMZN 10-K capital expenditure filings. Silicon represents approximately 55% of total CapEx; the remainder covers power, construction, and networking. Current total system CapEx is published on the Capital Ledger.

Revenue

Revenue Ledger

Entity-by-entity rollup sourced from earnings calls, SEC filings, and investor presentations. Revenue uses collected-revenue methodology — never run-rated or exit-ARR. Consumer vs. enterprise split informed by The Information reporting and company segment disclosures. Each entity carries its own provenance tier; current quarterly aggregate is on the Revenue Ledger.

Compute

Compute Ledger

Hyperscaler AI cloud + neocloud + Oracle OCI revenue, published both gross-disclosed and net of model-lab pass-through per the principal/agent verification gate (D6). Direct frontier-API revenue (OpenAI / Anthropic API direct) stays on the Revenue Ledger; only the cloud-margin leg is in Compute. Aggregator: scripts/derive_compute_revenue.py. Disclosures file: data/compute_disclosures.json. Current quarterly aggregate is on the Compute Ledger.

Usage

Usage Ledger

Multi-model consensus methodology tracking 14 providers. Primary anchors: OpenRouter public throughput statistics and NVIDIA inference throughput disclosures. China accounts for approximately 50% of global volume. No single provider discloses full usage data, so all figures are Med or Low confidence. Current daily-volume range is published on the Usage Ledger.

Compute Revenue — three-segment model & principal/agent gate

The Compute Ledger decomposes hyperscaler + neocloud AI revenue into three segments so the page can publish the right number for the right question. Headline aggregates are post-Copilot — Copilot-class revenue is per-seat productivity SaaS, scoped to a future Apps Ledger.

Three-segment model (plain-English naming locked 2026-05-06)

  • Frontier lab compute. Money frontier model labs (OpenAI, Anthropic, Mistral, Cohere, smaller labs) pay external compute providers (hyperscalers, neoclouds) to train and serve their own foundation models. Estimated bottom-up from per-lab revenue × cloud-mix (e.g. OpenAI ~80% Azure, Anthropic ~60% AWS / ~30–40% GCP TPUs). Some hyperscalers also hold equity stakes in the model labs they bill, which is why this line is sized and surfaced separately rather than netted into a single Compute number. Strict definition: only external lab → hyperscaler flows count. Internal compute — e.g. Google running Gemini training on Google Cloud, or Meta running LLaMA on its own datacentres — contributes $0. ~$36B 2025.
  • AI workload compute. AI-Native customers (running open or own models), enterprises (running internal AI workloads), model-serving infrastructure cos, and sovereign/regulated workloads buying raw cloud GPU/instance capacity. Residual after Frontier lab compute + Copilot deduction. ~$5B 2025.
  • Hosted model APIs. Bedrock + Azure OpenAI Service + Vertex partner-served — packaged token APIs, the cloud-margin leg of frontier-model resale. Pass-through to model labs applies here only. ~$4.5B gross 2025; ~$1B pass-through.

Pass-through rule. Pass-through is the lab rev-share applied to Hosted model APIs, not to total AI revenue. The wq-087 page applied pass-through % to gross AI revenue and reported ~$11.5B 2025 — overstated by ~10× because most of hyperscaler AI revenue is direct compute spend (Frontier lab + AI workload), not token-API resale. Corrected pass-through ~$1B 2025; corrected net ~$44.5B (sum-of-quarterlies post-Copilot basis, post-wq-091 segment sizing).

Copilot scope-out rule. M365 Copilot, GitHub Copilot, Copilot Studio (MSFT); Gemini-in-Workspace SKUs (GOOGL); equivalent embedded-AI-feature productivity lines elsewhere — these are per-seat SaaS, not Compute. They are deducted from each provider's disclosed AI line and tracked under copilot_excluded_*_usd_b for transparency. They flow to a future Apps Ledger when that is built; until then they are tracked but not published on the Compute page. AWS does not have a named productivity Copilot line; default deduction is 0 unless a specific embedded-AI line is identifiable in the 10-Q. Scope-out uses MS-declared values (not TAIL-derived) so the deduction matches what is in the reported AI run-rate.

Principal/agent verification gate (D6). Before publication, the revenue recognition policy notes from the most recent 10-K are read for AWS, MSFT (Azure), GOOGL (Cloud), and ORCL (OCI). For each, principal (gross) vs. agent (net) treatment under ASC 606 is confirmed. All four reviewed entities use principal treatment for AI reseller arrangements (verified 2026-05-06; documented in data/methodology_constants.json:compute_revenue.principal_agent_treatment). Treatment is re-verified each cycle as 10-Ks are refiled.

Direct frontier-API revenue is OUT of Compute (D3 wq-087). When Bedrock resells Claude, AWS keeps the cloud margin (Compute, Hosted model APIs net); Anthropic gets the rest (Model revenue). When a customer calls Anthropic directly, 100% is Model revenue with the underlying compute showing on AWS/GCP P&Ls as Frontier lab compute. This avoids ecosystem-level double-counting between Compute and Apps/Model Revenue.

Segment sizing methodology (locked wq-091, 2026-05-06)

The wq-089 ship used editorial AI-share weights (e.g. AWS AI = 15% of AWS revenue) to size each provider's AI line. The wq-091 correction replaces that with bottom-up segment sizing per the Final Locked Table in the derivation memo. Approach by provider:

  • Microsoft Azure AI ($28B 2025). Holds — Microsoft self-disclosed run-rate basis is the tightest of the four ($37B Q1 2026 annualised, +123% YoY). Frontier lab compute $18B (OpenAI ~80% Azure-resident); Hosted model APIs $1.5B (Azure OpenAI Service to enterprise — bottom-up at realistic token volumes); AI workload compute $1.1B; Copilot $7.4B (MS-declared seat-math: 15M paid M365 Copilot seats × $30/mo × 12 + GitHub Copilot $1.65B + sundry).
  • AWS AI ($10B 2025). Restated downward from $18B per Andy Jassy's 2026 shareholder letter disclosure of "$15B annualised AI run rate" Q1 2026 (Tier 1B CEO disclosure). Walking back to 2025 calendar via observed Anthropic growth shape: Q1 ~$1.5B, Q2 ~$2B, Q3 ~$2.75B, Q4 ~$3.5B. Frontier lab compute $7B (Anthropic on AWS infra ~$5–6B + minor labs $1–2B); Hosted model APIs $2.5B (Bedrock); AI workload compute $0.5B residual.
  • Google Cloud AI ($7B 2025). Restated downward from $18B per bottom-up build (no discrete GCP AI revenue figure disclosed). Anchored on Q2 2025 commentary "13 product lines now exceed $1B run-rate" — if 4–5 are AI-driven (Vertex AI Platform, Gemini API, AI Hypercomputer, AI agent/search products, AI-driven analytics) at $1.0–1.5B mid-2025 each, calendar 2025 lands $6–8B central $7B. Frontier lab compute $3.5B (Anthropic on TPUs $1.5–2B + OpenAI on GCP $0.5–1B partial-year + Mistral/Cohere/AI21 ~$1B); Hosted model APIs $0.5B (Vertex partner-served); AI workload compute $1.5B; Copilot $1.5B (Gemini-in-Workspace external Apps revenue, MS-declared analogue).
  • Oracle OCI AI ($3B 2025). Holds. Frontier lab compute $2B (OpenAI Stargate partial-year billings as DC capacity comes online); AI workload compute $1B; no Hosted model APIs; no Copilot.
  • Neoclouds (CoreWeave + Nebius + Lambda + Crusoe = $6.45B 2025). Holds aggregate. CoreWeave $4.5B is ~85% Microsoft-sub-rent (much of which is OpenAI workload — captured separately under msft_ai Frontier lab compute as the same flow under different ASC 606 principal-treatment legs). Smaller neoclouds: Mistral, Cohere, smaller labs.

The Anthropic equity gain on Alphabet's Q1 2026 P&L ($36.9B) is investment income, not Cloud operating revenue (per Fortune, 30 April 2026) — and does not inflate the GCP AI line. The CoreWeave figure includes ~$3.8B of Microsoft-sub-rented capacity that is also recognised as AWS / Azure compute revenue elsewhere; under each entity's revenue-recognition policy both legs are real revenue lines on each company's P&L, so the ecosystem total is gross of that overlap by definition. We surface it separately rather than try to net it out at the Compute layer.

Calendar basis: sum-of-quarterlies, not annualised run-rate (locked wq-092, 2026-05-06)

For each provider, the 2025 calendar AI line is published on a sum-of-quarterlies basis — the four reported (or derived) 2025 quarterly AI revenues summed. We chose this over the alternative annualised run-rate basis (year-end exit run-rate × 4), even where the run-rate number is the figure CEOs prefer to disclose. Why: in a year of accelerating growth, sum-of-Q is materially smaller than annualised — Microsoft's 2025 calendar AI is $25.25B sum-of-Q vs $28B annualised exit (~+10% gap); Amazon and Google sum-of-Q ≈ annualised because their growth shape doesn't diverge as much. If the trajectory chart anchored Q1 2026 at $9.25B (= disclosed $37B run-rate ÷ 4) but forced the four 2025 quarters to sum to $28B, Q4 2025 would have to balloon above $9.25B — producing a visible Q-o-Q drop into Q1 26 that contradicts the +123% YoY growth narrative.

Sum-of-quarterlies basis lets every major provider's quarterly trajectory grow monotonically into Q1 2026 without distortion. The annualised run-rate value is preserved as a separate context field per provider in data/compute_disclosures.json (value_2025_annualised_run_rate_basis_usd_b); we do not lose it. Layer Stack and trajectory chart use sum-of-quarterlies. See docs/briefs/wq-092-compute-ledger-trajectory-no-qoq-drops.md for full rationale.

Cohort vs ecosystem Apps Revenue — Layer Stack basis

The Layer Stack visual on /compute publishes cohort Apps Revenue (the Revenue Ledger totalCustomerRevenue, ~$17.36B as of 2026-05-06) — not an editorially-extended ecosystem total. The wq-087 page used a Tier 2A 5× multiplier ($17.4B → $100B) to bridge cohort to "enterprise SaaS AI ARR"; that multiplier had no provenance trail and the ratio swung ±25% on a single editorial constant. The corrected page shows the cohort number as-is and flags the cohort-vs-ecosystem gap as a future-Apps-Ledger scope. All four Layer Stack layers are on lookback 2025 actuals on the same time-basis (sum-of-quarterlies for compute, FY26 calendar 2025 for silicon) so the multipliers between layers are apples-to-apples.

Revenue Ledger — entity archetype taxonomy (wq-090)

The Revenue Ledger Sankey routes per-entity revenue across channels (Model Subs / Model API / Hyperscalers / AI Native Apps / Trad. SaaS) and into buyer segments (Consumer / AI Natives / Enterprises & Govs / VC-Investors). Pre-wq-090 the engine applied a flat 70/30 Model API / Hyperscalers split to every provider's API revenue. That weighting is roughly right for enterprise customers (who buy Bedrock / Azure OpenAI / Vertex for compliance and procurement) but wrong for AI Natives — Cursor, Glean, Perplexity, Harvey etc. go ~95% direct to OpenAI / Anthropic for cost and latency. Because the cohort is mostly AI Natives, the flat 30% Hyperscalers weight inflated the Hyperscalers channel ($3.63B at ship). wq-090 replaces the flat split with per-archetype weights so each entity routes through the channels its real customers actually use.

Every entity in the cohort now carries an entity_archetype field. The taxonomy:

Subscription revenue routes to Consumer; API revenue splits between AI Natives and Enterprises & Govs per archetype; enterprise contracts route to Enterprises & Govs.

Reconciliation against the Compute Ledger. Post-wq-090 the Revenue Ledger Hyperscalers channel lands at ~$1.7B (gross). After the 20% hyperscaler take-rate, that implies ~$1.36B of cohort lab revenue passing through Hyperscaler resale — comparable to the Compute Ledger Hosted-model-APIs pass-through (~$1B) within tier 2A noise. The remaining ecosystem Hosted-model-APIs gross (~$4.35B per wq-089) covers non-cohort enterprise spend (Fortune 500 buying Bedrock / Azure OpenAI outside the 9-source cohort) and is documented on the Compute Ledger but out of scope for the Revenue Ledger cohort.

ARR vs collected revenue — what each surface shows

The Ledger surfaces two distinct revenue measures for AI providers, and they are not interchangeable per SKILL.md hard rule #6. Mixing them is the most common mistake when reading frontier-AI financials.

MeasureDefinitionWhere it surfacesTime orientation
ARR (run-rate) Trailing-quarter revenue × 4. Forward-looking proxy for the next 12 months at the current pace. Usage Ledger hero stat ("Combined Provider ARR") and per-provider chart bars; provider tile data.rev in site-data.json. Forward-looking
Collected revenue (audited) GAAP-style revenue actually booked over a fiscal year. Backward-looking, audit-grade. Home page hero ("$22B of AI revenue earned, CY23–25") and Revenue Ledger sankey; per-provider year-keyed entries on /timeline. Backward-looking

The two measures typically diverge by 5–6× for frontier model providers because ARR captures the latest quarter annualised (currently growing rapidly) while collected revenue captures the audited annual figure (which lags). For example, OpenAI's reported ARR was around $25B (Q1 2026 run-rate) while its 2025 collected revenue was around $4.3B — the same company, two different numbers, both correct for what they measure. The Ledger keeps both and labels each surface explicitly.

Year-keyed audited revenue (e.g. Anthropic's 2026 collected revenue at $6B from the wq-028 P1.6 replay) surfaces on the timeline page's FY revenue section, not the dashboard tile, by editorial choice (wq-032 Phase 1B). The dashboard tile is intentionally ARR-only because the dashboard's purpose is "current state at run-rate"; year-keyed audited figures belong on the timeline where the time orientation is explicit.

Tier Classification (provenance taxonomy)

Every data point in the AI Ledger carries a provenance tier. This is the underlying classification — it tells you where the number came from and how much weight to give it. The single-axis pill above is derived from this.

TierLabelDefinition
1AAuthoritativeFirst-party disclosure — earnings report, regulatory filing, official press release
1BCorroboratedTwo or more independent reporting sources agreeing within ~15%
2ADerivedDeterministic calculation from Tier 1 inputs (e.g. subtracting known segments from a reported total)
2BTriangulatedValue squeezed by multiple Tier 1 anchors setting upper and lower bounds
3AAnchored projectionTrend extrapolation from a Tier 1 or Tier 2 base point

4EditorialRound-number guess — replace as soon as better data arrives

Modeling Principles

  • Changelog discipline: Any revision to a published estimate triggers a changelog entry with the old value, new value, and reason for the change. No silent edits.
  • Scenario parameters are authored, not computed: Bear, Base, and Bull scenarios use hand-picked growth rates and ratios (Tier 3C). They are editorial judgements, clearly labelled, not outputs of a forecasting model.
  • Source hierarchy is enforced: Higher-tier data cannot be overwritten by lower-tier data. A Tier 1A filing always takes precedence over a Tier 3A projection. The system enforces this programmatically.
  • Assumptions are documented: Every derivation step, growth rate, split ratio, and rounding decision is recorded in persistent assumption docs. If you can't trace a number back to its source, it gets flagged for review.
  • Replace Tier 4 aggressively: Tier 4 (Editorial) values exist only as placeholders. They are the first priority for upgrade whenever new data surfaces.

What We Don't Claim

Transparency about our limits is part of the methodology. These are known gaps that we track but do not claim to have resolved:

  • China-domestic usage below the top three: We track ByteDance, Baidu, and Tencent through public disclosures. Smaller Chinese labs and enterprise deployments are estimated, not measured.
  • We don't audit inference cost claims: When providers disclose cost-per-token or margin data, we triangulate against known infrastructure costs. We don't have access to verify these claims directly.
  • Private ARR for non-disclosing labs: Anthropic (pre-IPO), xAI, and others don't report revenue publicly. We use investor call commentary, court filings, and press reports — these carry Tier 1B or 2A confidence at best.
  • Idle capacity carries ±20% uncertainty: No hyperscaler discloses GPU utilisation rates. Our idle estimates are residuals after allocating to known workloads.
  • Token volume is a lower bound: Our estimates rely on public API throughput and disclosed metrics. Internal inference (not exposed via API) is not captured. Actual global volume is likely higher than our estimates.
